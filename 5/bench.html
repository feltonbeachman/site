Bench Press Guide

I love bench press. It’s so fun. Here’s how I bench:

1. Pad must be sticky, with give.
1.1 Two points of contact: traps and feet.
1.2 A sticky pad with give secures the traps and enables backward slide (see 11).

2. Hips higher than knees to maximize force transfer off the floor.

3. Hold bar with minimal grip to enable effective use of upper back musculature.
3.1 Chalk up hands and use bar with aggressive knurling.
3.2 I like bar at meaty base of palms, with wrists cocked back.
3.3 Hands get diagonal and turn towards each other on their own with bar at base of palms.
3.4 Bar will remain in place no matter what, even if you release fingers.
3.5 With minimal grip, let your back work on eccentric as opposed to arms.
3.6 Back is better suited to handling bar than arms.
3.7 Bar becomes an extension of the body.

4. Unrack with maximum leg drive and shoulder retraction and depression.
4.1 Leg drive creates back position that enables effective use of upper back musculature.
4.2 Shoulder retraction and depression enable effective use of upper back musculature.
4.3 Stack arms vertically under bar, meaning wrists and elbows along same vertical plane.
4.4 To stack arms under racked bar, you must flare the elbows.

5. Butt is high up from maximum leg drive. Extend the triceps to unrack.
5.1 Guide the bar forward by changing elbow angle, bringing elbows closer to chest.
5.2 Again, don't grip the shit out of the bar. Let your upper back work.
5.3 Move forward with locked arms until upper back feels secure.

6. Set your butt down.
6.1 Don't bear weight with your butt.
6.2 Bearing weight with butt kills force transfer off the floor.
6.3 Set butt down to where you're barely touching the pad. Let's call it "hover butt."
6.4 "Hover butt" is another way of describing persistent leg drive.
6.5 "Hover butt" ensures maximum force transfer off the floor and to the bar.
6.6 "Hover butt" maintains upper back position to enable effective use of upper back musculature.

7. Move the bar down.
7.1 Maintain the loosest possible grip.
7.2 Load your upper back on the eccentric. Use your upper back to move the bar.
7.3 The back position you developed in previous steps makes it easy to recruit upper back musculature.
7.4 Move bar down with intent, deliberately going as fast as possible.
7.5 This impacts the nervous system, improving its ability to recruit muscle fibers.
7.6 Fast eccentric also loads the stretch reflex, making the press easier.
7.7 Fast eccentric is a product of good positioning and effective use of upper back musculature.
7.8 Fast eccentric improves bar control given effects on nervous system that enhance muscle fiber recruitment.

8. Use a bar path that takes full advantage of leg drive and maximizes force transfer off the floor.
8.1 Leg drive is a horizontal force.
8.2 Create an efficient bar path by setting up the conditions for a backward press, not an upward press, which resists the horizontal force.
8.3 To do this, touch lower by bringing elbows closer to chest.
8.4 Maintain stacked arms under bar. Don't tuck elbows to where they're closer to chest than your wrists.
8.5 Distance between elbow and chest and wrist and chest must be the same.

9. Touch low, so long as you angle forearms backwards.
9.1 I touch as low as possible with backwards forearms.
9.2 Backwards forearms means elbows slightly in front of wrists, which are closer to rack than elbows.
9.3 This creates a pressing position in sync with leg drive, ready to launch back towards rack.
9.4 You don't want elbows behind wrists, to where elbows are closer to rack than wrists.

10. Press towards the rack to take full advantage of leg drive.
10.1 To move bar towards rack and in line with leg drive, which is a horizontal force, you must change elbow angle.
10.2 Go from elbows closer to chest at bottom, to flared elbows farther from chest at lockout.
10.3 Flaring elbows enables horizontal movement of bar, meaning backwards towards rack.
10.4 Not flaring elbows results in moving bar upwards and not backwards.
10.5 Moving upwards resists leg drive, sapping your power.

11. Don't dig in with the traps.
11.1 When pressing, you want to almost try to slide traps back, so to speak, as if you're trying to get your head to fall off the edge.
11.2 So it's as if you're trying to move the body back, and not down into the pad.
11.3 Digging into the pad saps your power.
11.4 By trying to move back, you're maximizing the force transfer off the floor and into the bar.
11.5 By working with a sticky pad with give, you can try to slide off as much as possible, and your traps will remain in place.
